The White Cliffs of Dover

The Calcium Carbonate Heritage of the Coast

By CATS Insights Editorial Team | October 2025
[ Placeholder Image: The White Cliffs ]

Introduction

The White Cliffs of Dover are perhaps the most iconic symbol of Kent, rising dramatically from the English Channel. But beyond their cultural significance, they represent a fascinating study in large-scale inorganic chemistry.

Formation: The Coccolithophore Legacy

The cliffs are primarily composed of chalk, a soft, fine-grained, white limestone. This chalk consists almost entirely of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). Around 70 million years ago, during the Late Cretaceous period, this region was submerged under a shallow, warm sea. Trillions of single-celled algae known as coccolithophores lived here. Upon dying, their calcium carbonate exoskeletons sank to the sea floor, slowly compressing over millions of years to form the massive chalk deposits we see today.

Chemical Erosion

The White Cliffs are retreating at an expanding rate due to both physical weathering and chemical erosion. Rainwater, which is naturally slightly acidic due to dissolved atmospheric carbon dioxide, reacts with the calcium carbonate.

CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(a) → Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq)

This reaction forms calcium bicarbonate, which is highly soluble in water and is washed away into the sea, literally dissolving the cliffs over time.

Thermal Decomposition

From an industrial perspective, the chalk of the White Cliffs (and surrounding Kent quarries) has historically been mined to produce quicklime (calcium oxide, CaO). This is achieved through thermal decomposition by heating the calcium carbonate to temperatures above 840°C in lime kilns.

CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

This quicklime was essential for creating lime mortar, which built much of historical Kent and London.

Environmental Significance & Kent Connection

As average global temperatures rise, increased atmospheric CO₂ leads to more acidic rainfall, accelerating the dissolution of the cliffs. The study of the White Cliffs is not just local history; it is a live observation of the carbon cycle and ocean acidification.
