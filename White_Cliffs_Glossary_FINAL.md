# White Cliffs Glossary — Final Version (based on published article)

This glossary covers every technical term, phrase, equation, and concept in the final version of "Written in White: The Chemistry of Dover's White Cliffs" that a general reader or A-Level history student would not be expected to know.

---

## Abstract / Introduction

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **English Channel** | The narrow body of water separating southern England from northern France, approximately 32 km wide at its narrowest point (the Strait of Dover). |
| **Chemical archive** | A metaphor: the cliffs preserve a record of ancient chemical processes in the same way a library preserves written records. The chemistry of the ancient ocean is 'frozen' in the rock. |
| **Marine carbon cycling** | The continuous movement of carbon through the ocean in different chemical forms — as dissolved CO₂, as carbonate and bicarbonate ions in seawater, and as solid calcium carbonate in shells and sediment. |
| **Crystalline sedimentary record** | Sedimentary rock (formed from accumulated deposits) in which the minerals have a crystal structure. Chalk is composed of tiny calcite crystals. |
| **Cretaceous ocean** | The global ocean during the Cretaceous period (145–66 million years ago). Sea levels were much higher than today, and large parts of what is now land were submerged. The word 'Cretaceous' comes from the Latin *creta*, meaning chalk. |
| **Atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO₂)** | A gas in the Earth's atmosphere. It dissolves in rainwater to form a weak acid, and its concentration affects global temperature and ocean chemistry. Current levels are approximately 425 parts per million (ppm). |

## I. Shells to Stone: Coccolith Sedimentation and Diagenesis

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Coccolithophores** | Microscopic single-celled algae (phytoplankton) that live in the upper layers of the ocean. They build tiny armour plates made of calcium carbonate (calcite) around their cells. They are among the most important organisms in the ocean carbon cycle and are responsible for building the chalk of the White Cliffs. |
| **Unicellular** | Consisting of a single cell, as opposed to multi-celled organisms like plants or animals. |
| **Phytoplankton** | Microscopic organisms that float in the upper ocean and photosynthesise (use sunlight to make energy), like plants do on land. They form the base of the marine food chain. |
| **Haptophyta** | The biological division (a major taxonomic grouping, similar to a phylum) to which coccolithophores belong. |
| **µm (micrometre)** | One millionth of a metre, or one thousandth of a millimetre. A human hair is about 70 µm wide, so coccolithophores (5–20 µm) are far too small to see with the naked eye. |
| **Calcite** | The most stable crystalline form of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). It is the mineral that makes up chalk, limestone, and marble. Calcite defines hardness 3 on the Mohs scale. |
| **Coccoliths** | The individual calcite plates that coccolithophores build and interlock to form a protective sphere (coccosphere) around the cell. Each plate is typically 1–5 µm across — about 50 times smaller than the width of a human hair. |
| ***Emiliania huxleyi*** | The most abundant coccolithophore species in modern oceans. Its blooms can be so large they are visible from space as milky-white patches in the sea. Named after Thomas Huxley and Cesare Emiliani. |
| ***Watznaueria* and *Prediscosphaera*** | Genera (groups) of coccolithophores that were dominant during the Cretaceous period. They are now extinct but their coccolith plates are preserved in chalk. |
| **Intracellular** | Occurring inside the cell. Coccolithophores build their calcite plates inside a specialised internal compartment, not on their outer surface. |
| **Golgi-derived vesicle** | A small membrane-enclosed sac produced by the Golgi apparatus (a cellular structure involved in packaging and transporting molecules). In coccolithophores, the coccolith vesicle is where the calcite plate is assembled. |
| **Coccolith vesicle** | The specific Golgi-derived compartment in which a single coccolith plate is constructed before being transported to the cell surface. |
| **Coccolith-associated polysaccharides (CAPs)** | Sugar-based molecules attached to the coccolith that help control where and how calcite crystals grow. They act as a biological 'template' that directs crystal formation into the correct shape. |
| **Nucleation** | The very first step of crystal formation, where a few ions come together to form a tiny 'seed' crystal. All subsequent crystal growth builds on this initial nucleus. |
| **Crystal morphology** | The shape and form of a crystal. CAPs control morphology so that coccoliths grow into their characteristic wheel-like shapes rather than random lumps. |
| **Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ → CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O** | The net chemical equation for calcification. Calcium ions (Ca²⁺) and bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) combine to form solid calcium carbonate (the coccolith), releasing carbon dioxide and water as by-products. |
| **Mole** | A standard unit in chemistry representing 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, or ions). One mole of calcite weighs approximately 100 grams. |
| **Carbon sink** | Any process or reservoir that removes carbon from the atmosphere and stores it. Building CaCO₃ shells locks carbon into solid form. |
| **Carbon source** | Any process that releases carbon (as CO₂) into the atmosphere. The calcification reaction releases one molecule of CO₂ for every molecule of CaCO₃ formed. |
| **Marine biogeochemistry** | The study of how chemical elements (especially carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus) cycle through living organisms, seawater, and marine sediments. |
| **Late Cretaceous period (100–66 Ma)** | The final stage of the Cretaceous period, ending with the mass extinction that killed the dinosaurs. 'Ma' means 'million years ago'. |
| **Epicontinental sea** | A shallow sea that floods a continental landmass, as opposed to a deep open ocean. Much of Europe was beneath such a sea during the Cretaceous. |
| **Palaeolatitude** | The latitude at which a location sat in the geological past. Due to tectonic plate movement, Kent was at roughly 35°N during the Cretaceous (it is now at approximately 51°N). |
| **ppm (parts per million)** | A unit of concentration. 425 ppm CO₂ means that for every million molecules of air, 425 are carbon dioxide. |
| **Terrigenous sediment** | Sediment derived from the erosion of land (soil, sand, rock fragments) washed into the sea by rivers. The Cretaceous flooding of the continent meant very little land-derived sediment reached the seabed, allowing almost pure coccolith accumulation. This explains chalk's exceptional purity. |
| **Coccospheres** | The complete sphere of interlocking coccolith plates surrounding a living coccolithophore cell. When the cell dies, the coccosphere breaks apart and the individual plates sink. |
| **Diagenesis** | The physical and chemical changes that transform loose sediment into solid rock after burial. For chalk, this mainly involves compaction (squeezing out water) and cementation (new calcite crystals growing between grains to glue them together). |
| **Overburden pressure** | The weight of all the sediment piled on top of a buried layer. This pressure squeezes out water and compacts the material beneath. |
| **Porosity** | The percentage of a rock's volume that is empty space (pores). Chalk has unusually high porosity (35–45%), meaning more than a third of its volume is air or water. This is one reason chalk is so soft and why it appears white. |
| **Flint** | A very hard, dark-coloured form of microcrystalline quartz (SiO₂) found as nodules and horizontal bands within chalk. It forms during diagenesis when dissolved silica migrates and re-crystallises. Neolithic humans mined chalk flint for tools. |
| **Radiolarians** | Microscopic marine zooplankton (animals) that build skeletons made of silica (SiO₂) rather than calcium carbonate. Their dissolved remains are the source of the silica that eventually forms flint in chalk. |
| **Sponge spicules** | Tiny needle-like structural elements made of silica that form the skeleton of certain sea sponges. Like radiolarian skeletons, they contribute silica to chalk deposits. |
| **Microcrystalline quartz** | Quartz (SiO₂) composed of crystals too small to see individually, even under a standard microscope. Flint is a type of microcrystalline quartz. |

## II. Why Chalk Is White: Light Scattering in Microcrystalline Calcite

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Iceland spar** | A naturally occurring, very pure and transparent variety of calcite, historically found in Iceland. It was famous for its double refraction (birefringence) — a single dot viewed through Iceland spar appears as two dots. |
| **Birefringence (double refraction)** | An optical property where a single ray of light entering a crystal is split into two rays travelling at different speeds and in slightly different directions. Calcite has very strong birefringence — the strongest of any common mineral. |
| **nm (nanometre)** | One billionth of a metre. Visible light has wavelengths of approximately 380 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red). Ultraviolet light is shorter; infrared is longer. |
| **Mie scattering** | A type of light scattering that occurs when light hits particles whose size is comparable to or larger than the wavelength of the light. Unlike Rayleigh scattering (which preferentially scatters short wavelengths and makes the sky blue), Mie scattering affects all wavelengths roughly equally, producing white light. Named after German physicist Gustav Mie, who described the mathematics in 1908. |
| **Wavelength-selective absorption** | When a material absorbs some colours of light but not others — this is what makes coloured objects appear coloured. Chalk does NOT do this; it scatters all colours equally, which is why it appears white rather than any particular colour. |
| **Diffuse, broadband reflectance** | Light that is scattered in all directions ('diffuse') across the full visible spectrum ('broadband'). This is what whiteness looks like when measured optically. |
| **Refractive index (n)** | A number describing how much light slows down when it enters a material. Air has n ≈ 1.00; water has n ≈ 1.33; calcite has n ≈ 1.49–1.66. Every boundary between materials with different refractive indices causes some light to be reflected and scattered. |
| **Mohs hardness** | A scale from 1 (softest, talc) to 10 (hardest, diamond) that measures how easily a mineral can be scratched. Calcite defines hardness 3 — it can be scratched by a copper coin but not by a fingernail. This is why chalk is soft enough to write with. |

## III. Dissolving the Cliffs: Carbonate Equilibria and Chemical Weathering

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Equilibrium reactions** | Chemical reactions that can proceed in both directions (forward and reverse) and eventually reach a balance point where the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal. Shown with the ⇌ symbol rather than a one-way arrow. |
| **Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)** | A weak acid formed when carbon dioxide dissolves in water. It is the reason that rainwater is naturally slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) even without pollution. The fizz in carbonated drinks is dissolved CO₂ forming carbonic acid. |
| **Diprotic acid** | An acid that can donate two hydrogen ions (H⁺) per molecule in two successive steps. Carbonic acid first loses one H⁺ to become bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻), then can lose a second to become carbonate (CO₃²⁻). |
| **K₁ (hydration constant)** | The equilibrium constant for CO₂ dissolving in water to form carbonic acid. K₁ = 3.4 × 10⁻² means only about 3.4% of dissolved CO₂ actually converts to H₂CO₃ at any given moment. |
| **Ka₁ and Ka₂ (acid dissociation constants)** | Numbers that describe how far each dissociation step proceeds. Small Ka values (like 4.3 × 10⁻⁷ and 4.7 × 10⁻¹¹) mean the acid only partially dissociates — most molecules remain intact. This is why carbonic acid is classified as a 'weak' acid. |
| **pKa values (6.37 and 10.33)** | The negative logarithm of Ka. pKa₁ = 6.37 means that at pH 6.37, exactly half of the carbonic acid has dissociated to bicarbonate. pKa₂ = 10.33 means at pH 10.33, half the bicarbonate has dissociated to carbonate. These values appear on Figure 3. |
| **pH** | A logarithmic scale measuring acidity. pH 7 is neutral; below 7 is acidic; above 7 is alkaline. Each unit change represents a tenfold change in hydrogen ion concentration. Rainwater at pH 5.6 has about 25 times more H⁺ ions than pure water at pH 7. |
| **Calcium bicarbonate (Ca(HCO₃)₂)** | A soluble compound formed when calcium carbonate dissolves in carbonic acid. Unlike solid CaCO₃ (which is insoluble), calcium bicarbonate dissolves in water and can be carried away by flowing water. This is how the cliffs are chemically eroded. |
| **Reversible equilibrium** | A reaction that can go forwards or backwards depending on conditions. Adding more CO₂ pushes chalk dissolution forwards; removing CO₂ pushes it backwards (causing calcite to re-precipitate as stalactites or limescale). |
| **Le Chatelier's principle** | A fundamental principle in chemistry: if a system at equilibrium is disturbed (e.g., by changing concentration, temperature, or pressure), it will shift to partially counteract the disturbance. Increasing atmospheric CO₂ shifts the carbonate equilibrium toward more dissolution of chalk. |
| **Partial pressure of CO₂** | The pressure that CO₂ alone would exert if it occupied the entire atmosphere. Currently about 0.04% of atmospheric pressure (425 ppm). Higher partial pressure means more CO₂ dissolves in water, making rain more acidic. |
| **Stalactites and stalagmites** | Mineral formations in caves. Stalactites hang from the ceiling; stalagmites grow up from the floor. They form when dissolved calcium bicarbonate in groundwater loses CO₂ (by degassing into cave air), causing calcite to precipitate out of solution — the reverse of chalk dissolution. |
| **Hard water** | Water containing high concentrations of dissolved calcium and magnesium ions. In Kent, groundwater percolates through chalk, dissolving calcium carbonate, making the water 'hard'. This causes limescale in kettles, pipes, and washing machines. |
| **Carbon cycle (lithospheric component)** | The part of the global carbon cycle involving carbon locked into rocks (the lithosphere). Chalk formation removes carbon from the ocean over millions of years; chalk dissolution returns it. This is much slower than the biological carbon cycle (days to years). |
| **Bjerrum plot** | The graph shown in Figure 3. It shows the percentage of each dissolved inorganic carbon species (CO₂, H₂CO₃, HCO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻) as a function of pH. The classic crossed curves show that bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) dominates at seawater pH (~8.1), while dissolved CO₂ dominates at acidic pH. |

## IV. Fire and Powder: Thermal Decomposition and Kent's Lime Industry

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Thermal decomposition** | A chemical reaction in which a compound breaks down into simpler substances when heated. Calcium carbonate decomposes into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide when heated above 840°C. |
| **CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)** | The decomposition reaction. (s) means solid; (g) means gas. Solid chalk is heated, producing solid quicklime and carbon dioxide gas that escapes into the air. |
| **ΔH = +178 kJ mol⁻¹** | The enthalpy change of the reaction. The '+' sign means the reaction is endothermic — it absorbs heat from its surroundings. 178 kilojoules of energy are needed to decompose one mole (100 g) of calcium carbonate. This is why lime kilns required sustained high temperatures. |
| **Partial pressure exceeds one atmosphere** | At 840°C, the CO₂ gas produced by decomposition builds up enough pressure to escape into open air. Below this temperature, the reaction cannot proceed to completion in an open kiln because the CO₂ pressure is too low to overcome atmospheric pressure. |
| **Dead-burnt lime** | Quicklime that has been heated too aggressively (above approximately 1,200°C), causing the calcium oxide to sinter (fuse together into a dense, unreactive mass). Dead-burnt lime will not react properly with water, making it useless for mortar. |
| **Quicklime (calcium oxide, CaO)** | The white solid produced when chalk or limestone is burned in a kiln. It is highly reactive and dangerous — it reacts vigorously with water and can cause severe chemical burns on contact with skin. |
| **Slaking** | The process of carefully adding water to quicklime to produce slaked lime (calcium hydroxide). The reaction is violently exothermic: the mixture can reach temperatures above 100°C and may boil and spit. |
| **ΔH = −65 kJ mol⁻¹** | The enthalpy change for slaking. The '−' sign means the reaction is exothermic — it releases heat. This is why quicklime mixed with water gets extremely hot and was historically used as a weapon (Greek fire) and to heat food in the field. |
| **Calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂, slaked lime)** | A white powder produced by adding water to quicklime. It is the active ingredient in lime mortar and lime plaster. It is alkaline (pH ~12.4 in solution). |
| **Lime mortar** | A building material made by mixing slaked lime with sand and water. Used for thousands of years to bind bricks and stones together. Unlike modern cement, lime mortar remains slightly flexible, 'breathes' (allows moisture to pass through), and slowly self-heals small cracks through continued carbonation. |
| **Carbonation** | The slow process by which calcium hydroxide in lime mortar reabsorbs CO₂ from the air and gradually converts back to calcium carbonate. This is what causes lime mortar to harden over months and years — it is literally turning back into chalk. |
| **Portland cement** | The modern cement (invented in 1824 by Joseph Aspdin in Leeds) that largely replaced lime mortar in construction from the 19th century onwards. It is stronger and sets faster, but more brittle, less breathable, and far more carbon-intensive to produce. |
| **Weald Clay** | A geological formation of heavy clay soil found across the Weald of Kent and Sussex. These soils are naturally acidic and historically required liming (adding calcium oxide) to raise their pH and support productive agriculture. |
| **Agricultural amendment** | Any substance added to soil to improve its chemical or physical properties. Quicklime raises soil pH (reduces acidity), making acidic soils more suitable for crops. |
| **North Downs** | A ridge of chalk hills running east–west across Kent and Surrey, from the Hog's Back near Guildford to the White Cliffs at Dover. The chalk of the North Downs is the same geological formation that forms the cliffs. |

## V. The Acid Test: Ocean Acidification and the Carbonate Saturation State

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Ocean acidification** | The ongoing decrease in ocean pH caused by the absorption of human-produced CO₂ from the atmosphere. The ocean has become approximately 26% more acidic since the Industrial Revolution. Important: the ocean is still alkaline (pH ~8.1), not acidic in absolute terms. 'Acidification' means it is becoming *less* alkaline, not that it has become acid. |
| **Anthropogenic** | Caused by human activity. Anthropogenic CO₂ emissions include those from burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), manufacturing cement, and deforestation. |
| **Buffered** | In chemistry, a buffered solution resists changes in pH when acid or base is added. Seawater is naturally buffered by the carbonate system: excess H⁺ ions react with CO₃²⁻ to form HCO₃⁻, partially absorbing the acid. However, this buffering consumes carbonate ions, reducing their availability for shell-building organisms. |
| **Logarithmic scale (pH)** | A mathematical scale where each unit represents a tenfold change. A pH decrease of 0.1 units (from 8.2 to 8.1) means H⁺ concentration has increased by about 26%, because 10⁰·¹ ≈ 1.26. A decrease of 1.0 unit would mean a tenfold (1,000%) increase in acidity. |
| **Calcite saturation state (Ω, omega)** | A number describing whether seawater conditions favour calcite formation (Ω > 1, supersaturated) or calcite dissolution (Ω < 1, undersaturated). It is the single most important number for predicting whether shell-building organisms can thrive. |
| **Ω = [Ca²⁺][CO₃²⁻] / Ksp** | The mathematical definition. [Ca²⁺] and [CO₃²⁻] are the actual concentrations of calcium and carbonate ions in seawater. Ksp is the solubility product — the concentration at which calcite is exactly at equilibrium between dissolving and precipitating. |
| **Solubility product (Ksp)** | A constant describing how much of a sparingly soluble salt will dissolve at a given temperature and pressure. For calcite, Ksp increases with pressure and decreases with temperature, which is why deep, cold ocean water dissolves calcite more readily than warm, shallow water. |
| **Supersaturated (Ω > 1)** | When seawater contains more dissolved calcium and carbonate ions than the equilibrium amount. Under these conditions, it is thermodynamically favourable (energetically 'downhill') for organisms to build CaCO₃ shells. |
| **Saturation horizons** | The ocean depth at which the saturation state Ω equals exactly 1. Above this depth, calcite is stable and organisms can build shells; below it, calcite dissolves. These horizons are moving closer to the surface (shoaling) as the ocean absorbs more CO₂ — by 30–200 metres since pre-industrial times. |
| **Shoaling** | Moving upward toward the surface. The calcite saturation horizon is 'shoaling' because ocean acidification is reducing carbonate ion concentrations in progressively shallower waters. |
| **Business-as-usual emission scenarios (RCP 8.5)** | Climate projections assuming that global CO₂ emissions continue at current rates without significant policy intervention. Under these scenarios, atmospheric CO₂ could reach approximately 800 ppm by 2100 (compared to ~425 ppm today and ~280 ppm pre-industrial). RCP stands for 'Representative Concentration Pathway'. |
| **RCP 4.5 (moderate mitigation)** | A scenario assuming moderate emissions reductions are achieved. Surface pH would stabilise at approximately 7.9 by 2100 under this pathway, compared to 7.7–7.8 under business-as-usual. |

## VI. The Archive, Revisited

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Silica remobilisation** | The process during diagenesis in which dissolved silica (from radiolarian and sponge remains) migrates through the chalk pore water and re-precipitates as flint nodules in a different location. |
| **Boundary conditions** | In science, the external constraints that define how a system behaves. The same carbonate chemistry operates today as in the Cretaceous, but under different boundary conditions (higher CO₂, lower pH, different temperature). Changing the boundary conditions changes the outcome. |
| **Biomineralisation** | The process by which living organisms produce minerals. Coccolithophore calcification, coral reef building, bone formation, and tooth enamel production are all examples of biomineralisation. |
| **Equilibrium thermodynamics** | The branch of chemistry and physics that predicts the direction and extent of chemical reactions based on energy considerations (enthalpy, entropy, free energy). It explains why calcite dissolves under some conditions and precipitates under others. |
| **Biogeochemistry** | The study of how biological, geological, and chemical processes interact to cycle elements (especially carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and silicon) through the Earth system — atmosphere, ocean, land, and rock. |
