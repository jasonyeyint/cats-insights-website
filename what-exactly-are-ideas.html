What exactly are ideas?

The concept of "idea" in Deleuzian philosophy

By Luca Candelot

Philosophy  |  8 min read  |  The Worthgate School, CATS Insights

Abstract

Despite many attempts to understand and conceptualize the idea, this article finds in Deleuze the answer to the question put in the title. Deleuze understands the idea to be not part of essence but a multiplicity in which it can not be defined as a sole one, but rather as a complex system of problems, in which the idea itself is a problem.

"There's no need to fear or hope, but only to look for new weapons."

— Gilles Deleuze

1. Introduction

Since the early prolegomena of philosophy, in Plato's Theory of Forms, the concept of Idea has been widely debated. Over the course of history, many conceptions and misconceptions of what an Idea is have been elaborated. However, with the development of dialectics - more specifically the system developed by Immanuel Kant and Georg Wilhelm Hegel -, the debate towards what is the Idea gained a much more complex stance, which finds a synthesis with the French philosopher Gilles Deleuze.

This article will aim to explain his theory and foremost demonstrate the mode in which Deleuze, by his own Theory of Ideas, dialogues critically with past conceptualizations, formulating a new becoming to dialectics.

2. Background

In his book, Difference and Repetition1, Deleuze synthesizes his own conceptual understanding of what are Ideas. However, his work is primarily based upon previous elaborations done by Immanuel Kant throughout his transcendental dialectics.

For Kant, the Idea is an a priori form of reasoning. An idea is something that goes beyond the empirical and firms itself solely by reason. In such a sense, it is a pure ontoepistemological object that is a thing-by-itself. Ideas are influenced by the externality of sensibility however it is also an understanding between different coherences of synthetical judgements, which lay early foundations for Deleuze's concept of Rhizome.

"A conception formed from notions, which transcends the possibility of experience, is an idea, or a conception of reason."

— Kant, CPR, p. 222

So, in such a manner it is possible to argue how the concept of idea is intrinsically linked to transcendental dialectics, since the idea itself can not be synthetically thought of as a mean or an object not by itself. However, further developments will reveal the idea's rhizomatic properties.

2.1 The notion of problem

Gilles Deleuze defines the concept of the problem in a strongly unique way. Problems are universal dialectical inquiries. For example, Deleuze puts that the field of mathematics is a field of solutions, it does not deal with problems.2 This outlines the non-specific aspect of a problem - it may be solved through many approaches.

Furthermore, when there is a solution upon a problem, the problem is not eliminated, the solution only updates one of its various possibilities. It is thought of as a dialectical multiplicity.

2.2 The concept of multiplicity

Throughout the totality of his philosophy, Deleuze will use the concept of multiplicities to explain an important part of his reasoning. The concept was developed firstly by the mathematician Bernhard Riemman and then explored in philosophy by Henri Bergson, whose work was a major inspiration for Deleuze. The multiplicity relates to the complete opposition of the one and the many ones. Multiplicities can never be thought of as unitary manifestations or even as the relation of one with another, they are beyond that in such a sense that each relation is integrated with various multiplicities.

"In other words, the very notion of multiplicity taken as a substantive implies a displacement of all of thought: for the dialectical opposition of the one and the many, we substitute the typological difference between multiplicities."3

— Deleuze, 1969

As a conclusion, multiplicities must obey the following structure:

  1. Its elements must not possess a sensible form, nor conceptual meaning. Must not possess actual existence and are inseparable from a virtual potence. They must not have any previous identity. Just then, through undetermination, it becomes possible the manifestation of the free difference of the identity of the concept.
  2. The elements must be defined reciprocally by relations who must not subsist their independence. Those relations form a juxtaposition of elements who create an ideal connection.4
  3. Multiple ideal connections to the differential relationship. Multiplicities must update themselves in space-time correlations.

3. Deleuze's conception of the Idea

For Deleuze, Kant is the utmost basis to think about the Idea. In his book Difference and Repetition, Deleuze dedicates an entire chapter for this discussion.5 In his own elaboration, ideas are not essence, in such a way that they are formed by a cogito.6 They are not constituted by a totality of the object but for a rather complex structure of the problem in a broader sense. In such a way, the conceptualization of the problem by itself leads to formulating the boundaries and properties of the Idea.

Hence, problems are ideas.7 Problems are dialectical, they develop themselves by the Aufheben8 of the various multiplicities that concern the problem. The same process is evident for the Idea. However, there is a teleological difference between the problem and the Idea; problems have an isomorphic relationship. To every problem, it is expected to exist a solution, independently whether it is known to man or not. Regarding the Idea, Kant would argue that ideas are "problems to which there is no solution"9. Deleuze would therefore put that true problems are ideas, since their ontological affirmation does not rely on the answer, and they will continue to exist in spite of having no solution. Due to this specific sense, they are mutually immanent and transcendental.

Upon this notion, Deleuze put forward a general conceptualization for the Idea: "An idea is an n-dimensional, continuous, defined multiplicity"10. An idea would rely upon many conditions for it to happen as a phenomenon - n-dimensions. These n-dimensions always correlate with each other in different modes of relation - continuity. Finally, the idea cannot be changed unless there is a change to its multiplicity. Thus, the multiplicities of the idea acts as transcendental delimitators and imposed definitions for its own conceptualization.

Further on, the Idea obeys an internal temporality, split between three moments of synthesis. The moments go through the indeterminate until it finds the pseudo-concrete on the determinate. To explain this movement of the Idea, Deleuze used Kant's three moments of the Cogito11, which can be represented as:

  1. "I am" as undetermined
  2. Time appears as the moment in which existence is determinable
  3. "I think" as the determined

However, when adapting Kant's structure, Deleuze noticed that the moments of the Idea could also be thought of in the terms of differential calculus. For Deleuze, the indeterminate moment of the idea would be the differentiation in itself, notably dx, dy; while the movement is represented by dx/dy and finally the determinate are the values that are concluded from dx/dy. "dx is the Idea - the Platonic, Leibnizian or Kantian Idea, the 'problem' and its being."12 Therefore, dx represents the idea in its first dialectical movement, which develops itself further until the concrete as a solution for dx/dy.

In simpler terms, dx is the infinitesimal variation of x, while dx/dy is the quotient for its variation, the instantaneous rate of change in y in relation to x. This means that dx/dy represents no specific quantity, it is undeterminable by itself as it only shows an infinitesimal relationship. When dx, as the first undetermined, becomes a relation with dy, they become a multiplicity in totality. Any change to dx in even the slightest intensity impacts dy. In such a basis, it becomes evident how ideas are determined by multiplicities, towards a specific extent where it concatenates into a determinable.

4. Conclusion

In synthesis, Deleuze offers a new approach to the conceptualization of the Idea. Deleuze does not - as Kant did - understand ideas only as a pure concept of reason. For him, ideas can not be formed by essence but by the cogito in itself.

Understanding the concept of Idea is truly crucial in order to grasp the whole of Deleuzian's philosophy. It is from the Idea that Deleuze will elaborate on the theme of rhizome and multiplicities. By demonstrating that every idea is a multiplicity, Deleuze puts forward the notion of interdependence throughout reasoning in itself.

Thus, it is foremost important that one who wishes to comprehend contemporary philosophy is able to understand what is the idea and how the idea must not be thought as a thing-by-itself, but rather as various possibilities of the problematic, formulating a dialectical relationship towards the aims of rhizomatic exposure of multiplicities.
